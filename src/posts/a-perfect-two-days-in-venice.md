<p>Venice rewards people who walk it slowly and cross to the far side of things. Two days is enough to see the art that made the city famous, climb for the views, and get out onto the lagoon. Start early, before the day-trip crowds thicken, and let the bridges do the navigating.</p>

<p>Begin at the <strong>Rialto Bridge</strong>, the oldest and most famous span across the Grand Canal. Completed in 1591, it's a single marble arch with rows of shops built into both sides. The best view is from the water, so if you catch a <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="vah-poh-REH-toh">vaporetto</span> along the canal, watch for it. Cross over and head into the quieter lanes of San Polo.</p>

<p>A short walk brings you to the <strong>Frari Basilica</strong>, a huge Gothic Franciscan church that feels almost bare until you look up. Above the main altar hangs Titian's Assumption of the Virgin, one of the greatest altarpieces ever painted. The church also holds a Bellini triptych and the tomb of the sculptor Canova. It's a lot of masterwork under one brick roof.</p>

<p>From there, work south toward the Grand Canal and <strong>Ca' Rezzonico</strong>, a palazzo that now houses the Museum of 18th-Century Venice. The <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="tee-EH-poh-loh">Tiepolo</span> ceiling frescoes are the draw, along with period rooms and <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="LONG-ghee">Longhi</span>'s paintings of everyday Venetian life. It's a good look at how the city's wealthy actually lived.</p>

<p>Next is the <strong>Gallerie dell'Accademia</strong>, Venice's main art museum and the deepest collection of Venetian painting anywhere. Bellini, Titian, Veronese, <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="tin-toh-REH-toh">Tintoretto</span>, <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="kah-nah-LEH-toh">Canaletto</span>, five centuries of it. Give it more time than you think you'll need.</p>

<p>Just along the water sits the <strong>Peggy Guggenheim Collection</strong>, a jump of a few hundred years into modern art. Peggy Guggenheim lived in this Grand Canal palazzo, and the walls hold Picasso, Pollock, Dalí, and Magritte. The sculpture garden looks out over the water and makes a fine pause.</p>

<p>End the day at the <strong>Basilica di <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="SAN-tah mah-REE-ah della sah-LOO-teh">Santa Maria della Salute</span></strong>, the Baroque church whose dome guards the entrance to the Grand Canal. It was built as a vow of thanks after the plague, and entry is free. Inside are works by Titian and Tintoretto. As the light drops, this is one of the better places in Venice to stand still for a while.</p>

<p>Make for Piazza San Marco early. The <strong>St. Mark's Campanile</strong> is the tallest structure in the city at 98.6 meters. Here's a detail worth knowing: the original tower collapsed entirely in 1902, and what stands today is an exact replica finished by 1912. An elevator runs to the top for a wide look over the lagoon and rooftops.</p>

<p>Back at ground level, step into <strong>St. Mark's Basilica</strong>. The Byzantine interior is covered in golden mosaics, roughly 8,000 square meters of them across the ceiling and walls. Entry to the basilica is free; the <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="PAH-lah DOH-roh">Pala d'Oro</span> altarpiece and the upstairs museum cost EUR 3. Look up and let your eyes adjust to the gold.</p>

<p>Next door is the <strong>Doge's Palace</strong>, seat of Venice's rulers for 700 years. Inside hangs Tintoretto's Paradise, the largest oil painting in the world. The route takes you across the <strong>Bridge of Sighs</strong>, the enclosed white limestone bridge linking the palace to the old prison. It's named for the sighs of prisoners who caught their last glimpse of Venice through its stone-grilled windows before being led to the cells. You'll walk those cells too.</p>

<p>For the afternoon, take a vaporetto across to <strong>San Giorgio Maggiore</strong>, on its own island facing the piazza. <span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="pah-LAH-dee-oh">Palladio</span> designed the 16th-century basilica, and Tintoretto's Last Supper hangs inside. Ride the elevator up the 75-meter bell tower. Many people rate this the best panorama of Venice, partly because it's the one view that includes St. Mark's itself.</p>

<p>If you have time and energy left, head to <strong><span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="fon-dah-MEN-teh NOH-veh">Fondamente Nove</span></strong> and catch the vaporetto out to <strong><span class="wx-say" tabindex="0" data-say="boo-RAH-noh">Burano</span></strong>, about 45 minutes across the lagoon. It's a fishing village known for its rows of rainbow-colored houses and a long tradition of lace-making. It's one of the most photographed places in Italy, and it makes a calm counterweight to the density of central Venice.</p>

<h2>A few practical notes</h2>

<p>Venice is a city of bridges and steps, and most of them are not step-free, so factor that in if stairs are a problem. Vaporetto rides are the easy way between the sightseeing clusters and the only way out to San Giorgio and Burano. In summer the piazza fills fast, so front-load the marquee stops; in winter the light is low and the crowds thin, but the lagoon crossings get breezy, so bring a layer.</p>

<p>Wear shoes you can walk all day in, and keep the vaporetto timetable handy for the Burano leg so you don't get stranded waiting.</p>
