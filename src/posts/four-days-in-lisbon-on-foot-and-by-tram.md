<p>Lisbon spreads across seven hills above the Tagus, which means a good day here mixes flat downtown blocks with a funicular or two and a payoff view at the top. Four days gives you the classic core, the monuments out at Belém, the Alfama tangle, and a run to the coast. Here's how the days lay out.</p>

<h2>Day 1 — Baixa, Chiado, And The Bairro Alto Edge</h2>

<figure>

  <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Pra%C3%A7a_de_D._Pedro_IV.jpg" alt="Rossio Square" loading="lazy">

  <figcaption>Rossio Square — <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Pra%C3%A7a_de_D._Pedro_IV.jpg">Thomas from Vienna, Austria · CC BY 2.0</a></figcaption>

</figure>

<p>Start in the grid of the lower town at Rossio Square (Praça Dom Pedro IV). It's been Lisbon's main square since the Middle Ages, and the ground itself is worth a look: wave-pattern Portuguese calçada, twin baroque fountains, and the National Theatre at the north end.</p>

<p>Walk a couple of minutes south to the Santa Justa Lift on Rua do Ouro. This wrought-iron elevator dates to 1902 and was designed by an apprentice of Gustave Eiffel, which explains the look. It carries you up to Chiado level and a panoramic top platform.</p>

<p>Up top you're steps from the Carmo Convent on Largo do Carmo. The roof came down in the 1755 earthquake and was never rebuilt, so you walk under open sky between Gothic arches. It's now an archaeological museum overlooking the Baixa.</p>

<p>Drop into A Brasileira do Chiado on Rua Garrett, an art-nouveau café from 1905. The poet Fernando Pessoa was a regular; a bronze statue of him still sits at a sidewalk table out front. From there it's a short walk to Luís de Camões Square, the hinge between Chiado and Bairro Alto, centered on an 1867 bronze of the epic poet clutching his Lusíadas.</p>

<p>Head down to the river for Praça do Comércio, the vast arcaded square rebuilt after the earthquake and crowned by the triumphal Rua Augusta Arch. To finish, ride the Glória Funicular up from Restauradores. It's been climbing this slope since 1885 and drops you at the São Pedro de Alcântara Viewpoint, a two-tier garden terrace with a tile panel that names the hills and monuments spread out in front of you.</p>

<h2>Day 2 — Belém</h2>

<figure>

  <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/The_Jer%C3%B3nimos_Monastery_or_Hieronymites_Monastery.png/1280px-The_Jer%C3%B3nimos_Monastery_or_Hieronymites_Monastery.png" alt="Jerónimos Monastery" loading="lazy">

  <figcaption>Jerónimos Monastery — <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:The_Jer%C3%B3nimos_Monastery_or_Hieronymites_Monastery.png">Heartshade · CC BY 4.0</a></figcaption>

</figure>

<p>Belém sits west of the center along the river, and it holds most of Lisbon's grand monuments. Begin at the Jerónimos Monastery on Praça do Império, a late Gothic Manueline complex King Manuel I commissioned in 1501. Vasco da Gama and Luís de Camões are both buried here.</p>

<p>A short walk away is Pastéis de Belém on Rua de Belém, open since 1837 and still baking the original secret-recipe pastel de nata. You can watch the bakery through a window while you wait. Expect a line; move quickly and eat one warm.</p>

<p>Walk to the water for the Monument to the Discoveries, a 52-meter caravel- shaped monument from 1960 led by a statue of Prince Henry the Navigator. Continue along the riverfront to Belém Tower, the 16th-century Manueline fortress that guarded the harbor and sent explorers off down the Tagus.</p>

<p>Doubling back inland, the National Coach Museum on Avenida da Índia holds one of the best collections of royal coaches and carriages anywhere, spanning the 16th to 19th centuries. Close the day at MAAT, the wave-shaped riverside museum that opened in 2016. Its rooftop is walkable, so you can climb up for a last look at the river.</p>

<h2>Day 3 — Tram 28 And The Alfama Hills</h2>

<figure>

  <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Igreja_e_Mosteiro_de_S%C3%A3o_Vicente_de_Fora.jpg/1280px-Igreja_e_Mosteiro_de_S%C3%A3o_Vicente_de_Fora.jpg" alt="Monastery of São Vicente de Fora" loading="lazy">

  <figcaption>Monastery of São Vicente de Fora — <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Igreja_e_Mosteiro_de_S%C3%A3o_Vicente_de_Fora.jpg">Deensel · CC BY 2.0</a></figcaption>

</figure>

<p>Today runs on the yellow Tram 28, and the eastern terminus at Praça Martim Moniz is where to catch it before it fills. The line rumbles through Graça, Alfama, Baixa, Chiado, and beyond.</p>

<p>Ride or climb up to Miradouro da Senhora do Monte, the highest viewpoint in central Lisbon, looking across the seven hills to the castle and the river. Down the slope is the Graça Viewpoint, a shaded pine terrace beside the Graça church with a café kiosk and a good angle on the castle.</p>

<p>Work east to the Monastery of São Vicente de Fora, a 17th-century Mannerist building with tiled cloisters and the royal pantheon of the Braganza dynasty. Next door is the National Pantheon, the baroque former church of Santa Engrácia now serving as a mausoleum; the fado singer Amália Rodrigues rests here.</p>

<p>Drop into Alfama proper at Miradouro das Portas do Sol, the terrace that looks over terracotta rooftops tumbling to the water, with a statue of São Vicente and a kiosk. Down in the lanes, the Fado Museum at Largo do Chafariz de Dentro traces the history of fado, the music UNESCO recognizes as intangible heritage. Finish uphill at São Jorge Castle, the Moorish-era fortress dating to the 11th century, with wide views over the city and the Tagus. Note the streets around Alfama are steep and cobbled.</p>

<h2>Day 4 — The Coast At Cascais</h2>

<figure>

  <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Cascais_-_Santa_Marta_%2853854018150%29.jpg/1280px-Cascais_-_Santa_Marta_%2853854018150%29.jpg" alt="Cascais" loading="lazy">

  <figcaption>Cascais — <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Cascais_-_Santa_Marta_(53854018150).jpg">Jorge Franganillo · CC BY 2.0</a></figcaption>

</figure>

<p>For the last day, head 30 km west to Cascais, a former fishing village turned resort town with beaches, a marina, the Citadel Palace, and old-town cafés for lunch.</p>

<p>Just outside town is Boca do Inferno, a sea-eroded cliff arch where the Atlantic pounds into a collapsed grotto. Odd footnote: the occultist Aleister Crowley faked his own death here in 1930. Finish north of Cascais at Guincho Beach, a wild dune beach inside the Sintra-Cascais Natural Park. It's a serious windsurfing and kitesurfing spot, and it stood in for a chase in the Bond film On Her Majesty's Secret Service. Wind off the ocean is the norm here, so bring a layer.</p>

<p>Practical tip: wear shoes with grip. Lisbon's calçada pavement gets slick, and the Alfama and Graça climbs are steep.</p>
